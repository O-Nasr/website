01

The Core Concept

The most important thing to internalize about this topic: Microservices are not better than monoliths. They are a tradeoff. You gain independence and scale. You pay with complexity and distributed systems problems. Every interview answer on this topic must reflect that balance.

Monolith
One deployable unit. All modules share the same process, memory, and database. You deploy everything together.
Single deploy
Microservices
Many small, independent services. Each owns its data, deploys separately, and communicates over a network.
Independent deploys
⚠️
Interview trap: Never say "microservices are better." Say "microservices trade simplicity for independence." Interviewers want to see that you understand the cost.
02

What a Monolith Looks Like

A monolith isn't bad code — it's a single deployable unit. All modules (Users, Orders, Payments, Inventory) live in the same process. When Orders calls Payments, it's a direct in-process method call — no network, no latency, no failure modes.

C# — In-process call (monolith)
// Orders calling Payments — direct method call, no network
public class OrderService
{
    private readonly PaymentsModule _payments; // injected, same process

    public async Task PlaceOrderAsync(Cart cart)
    {
        var order = await CreateOrderAsync(cart);
        await _payments.ChargeAsync(cart.Total); // in-process: no latency, no failure
        await _inventory.ReserveAsync(cart.Items);
    }
}
03

The Microservices Split

Each module becomes a separate service with its own process, database, and deployment. When Orders calls Payments, it crosses a network. That single change creates all the complexity of microservices.

💡
The fundamental shift: When Orders calls Payments, it's now a network call. That one change creates all the complexity we study in Topics 2–11: retries, circuit breakers, service discovery, sagas, distributed tracing, and more.

The Tradeoff Table

Monolith wins on simplicity; microservices wins on independence. Neither wins on everything. Memorize this for interviews.

ScalingScale the whole appScale only hot services

Failure isolationOne bug can crash allFailures stay contained
Data consistencyEasy — one transactionHard — distributed txns

05

When to Break a Monolith

The answer interviewers want: not immediately, and not just because it's trendy. Break it when you hit a specific pain that microservices solves. The MonolithFirst pattern (Martin Fowler) — start with a well-structured modular monolith, split when you feel real pain.

The 4 Real Triggers

Deployment bottleneck
10 teams stepping on each other trying to deploy at the same time. A small bug fix in checkout requires a full-app deploy and a release window.
Scaling mismatch
Your search service gets 100× more traffic than your admin panel, but you're forced to scale both because they're the same binary.
Technology divergence
Your ML team needs Python, your core team uses C#, your mobile backend needs Node. A monolith locks you to one.
Team ownership
At 200 engineers, no one knows who owns what. Services give clear boundaries: the Orders team deploys the Orders service.
06

Decomposition Strategies

Three ways to split. Interviewers will ask which one and why.

07

The Strangler Fig Pattern

You never rewrite a live monolith from scratch — that's a death march. Instead, peel off one service at a time, route traffic to it, and retire that piece from the monolith. The monolith shrinks over months, not in one big bang.

08

Right-Sizing Services

This trips many candidates. The answer has a name: the two-pizza rule (Amazon). A service should be owned by a team small enough to be fed by two pizzas (~6–8 people). One business capability end to end — not one function, not one table.

Service sizing examples
// ❌ Too big — this is just a monolith
OrdersAndPaymentsAndInventoryService

// ✅ Just right — one business capability end to end
OrdersService  // create, read, cancel, history

// ❌ Too small (nano-services) — chatty, fragile coupling
CreateOrderService
CancelOrderService
UpdateOrderStatusService
⚠️
Nano-service danger: Going too small gives you massive network chattiness and tight operational coupling. You've added all the pain of microservices with none of the independence gain.
09

The Distributed Monolith Anti-Pattern

This is the worst of both worlds. Looks like microservices (many services, over a network) but behaves like a monolith — services are tightly coupled, must deploy together, or share a database.

Distributed monolith (bad)
Services exist but they share a database, must deploy together, or can't function without each other. You get all the operational complexity of microservices with none of the independence benefits.
Avoid this
True microservices (good)
Each service can deploy independently, owns its own database, and fails gracefully without taking down others. The network boundary is a real isolation boundary.
Target this
10

Interview Q&A

Would you start a new project with microservices?
No, usually not. Start with a well-structured monolith — a "modular monolith" — where modules have clean boundaries. Migrate to services when you hit concrete pain: scaling mismatch, deployment bottleneck, or team growth. This is Martin Fowler's MonolithFirst pattern.
What is the biggest advantage of microservices over a monolith?
Independent deployability. Each team can deploy their service without coordinating with others. This enables true continuous delivery at scale — 10 teams can each ship 10 times per day independently. Second is independent scalability — you scale only the bottleneck service, not everything.
What is the biggest challenge of microservices?
Data consistency. In a monolith, you wrap everything in a transaction and either it all commits or all rolls back. In microservices, you have no distributed transaction. You must handle partial failures, eventual consistency, and compensating transactions manually. This is what the Saga pattern (Topic 6) solves.
What is a distributed monolith?
A system that looks like microservices (many services over a network) but behaves like a monolith — services must deploy together, share a database, or can't function independently. You get all the operational complexity of microservices with none of the independence benefits. The worst of both worlds.
What is the Strangler Fig pattern?
An incremental migration strategy — you don't rewrite the monolith all at once (that's a death march). Instead, you peel off services one by one, redirect traffic to each new service, and retire that piece from the monolith. The monolith shrinks gradually over months until it's fully replaced or reduced to a small core.
How do you decide how big a microservice should be?
One business capability end to end. Not one function (too small — creates chatty nano-services) and not multiple capabilities (too big — defeats independence). Use the two-pizza rule: a team small enough to be fed by two pizzas (~6–8 people) should own the service. If a single change requires modifying multiple services, your boundaries may be wrong.
